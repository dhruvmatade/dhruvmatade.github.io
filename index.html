About

I am a PhD Scholar at Dublin City University specializing in Finance and Family Business research. My research focuses on financial decision-making in family firms, particularly examining how varying levels of family ownership influence capital structure decisions. I explore the critical role of institutional quality in shaping financing choices between debt and equity options.

My work also investigates how family firms contribute to democratic processes and examines the distinctive behavioral patterns of family businesses in the Global South, where economic and institutional contexts create unique challenges and opportunities.

Beyond academia, I maintain interests in chess, cricket, geopolitics, and cinema, which provide diverse perspectives that enrich my analytical approach to business and finance research.

Research Interests

  • Financial decision-making in family firms
  • Capital structure decisions and family ownership levels
  • Institutional quality and financing choices (debt vs. equity)
  • Family firms and democratic contributions
  • Family business behavior in Global South markets

Research Interest

Family Firms in Emerging Markets: A Different Paradigm

One of the most fascinating aspects of my research is how family firms in the Global South operate under fundamentally different institutional constraints compared to their Western counterparts. While Western literature often views family ownership as a potential agency problem, my preliminary findings suggest that in emerging markets, family control may actually serve as a substitute for weak institutional frameworks...

The Democracy-Family Business Nexus

An intriguing question that emerged during my literature review: How do family businesses contribute to democratic stability? Initial evidence suggests that family firms, with their long-term orientation and community embeddedness, may serve as important stabilizing forces in developing democracies...
